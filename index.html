It's a Systemic Problem

We often blame problems on "the system." But what exactly is the system? And which part of the system is causing the problem?

That's the key characteristic of systemic problems: often, none of the individual parts are broken. The issue lies in how these parts interact, the overall outcomes the system produces, and the lack of considerations or forsight when the system was designed.

What is a System?

A system is a collection of interconnected parts working together towards a common purpose. Think of a car: the engine, transmission, wheels, and steering all function independently, but they must interact seamlessly for the car to drive. Systems exist everywhere, from natural ecosystems to social structures.

What is a Problem?

A problem is a situation, condition, or issue that is unsatisfactory, undesirable, or harmful. It represents a deviation from a desired state or outcome and typically requires action or intervention to resolve or solution.

What is a Systemic Problem?

A systemic problem is an issue that's baked into the structure of a system, leading to recurring negative outcomes. Imagine a factory where workers are constantly getting injured. A systemic problem might be a lack of safety training or poorly maintained equipment - issues that affect the whole system and lead to repeated accidents, rather than a single incident caused by one worker's mistake.

Examples

  • The Gender Pay Gap:

    Disenfranchised Group: Women

    Problem*: Overall, women are paid less than men. On average, women working full-time, year-round are paid 84% of what men are paid. In other words, the typical woman working full-time would need to work from January 1, 2023, until March 12, 2024, to make what the typical man working full-time made in 2023.

    Common Misconception: It's often assumed that the gender pay gap is simply a matter of women being paid less for the same work, but it's more nuanced than that. Factors like occupational segregation (where women are concentrated in lower-paying fields) and the undervaluing of female-dominated professions contribute significantly. Additionally, implicit biases in hiring and promotion processes can subtly disadvantage women even when there's no overt intention to discriminate.

    Roadblocks to Progress: Closing the gap requires addressing deeply ingrained societal biases, changing corporate cultures, and potentially legislative action regarding pay transparency and equal pay for equal work. These changes are complex, encounter resistance, and take time to implement effectively.

    *Department of Labor - What you need to know about the gender wage gap

  • Climate Change:

    Disenfranchised Group: Marginalized communities and future generations.

    Problem*: The Earth's climate is changing at an alarming rate, leading to rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and disruptions to ecosystems.

    Common Misconception: While individual actions are important, the problem is primarily systemic, driven by a reliance on fossil fuels, unsustainable industrial practices, and a lack of global cooperation. Many people acknowledge climate change but feel that actions they themselves can take are too small to make a difference.

    Roadblocks to Progress: Requires global cooperation to transition away from fossil fuels, adopt sustainable practices, and invest in climate mitigation and adaptation. This involves overcoming political hurdles, economic interests, and differing priorities among nations, making a rapid and unified solution difficult. Individually it can seem helpless to some, as they don't witness the impact their individual contributions might make.

    *Environmental Protection Agency - Climate Change Research

  • Healthcare Inequality:

    Disenfranchised Group: Low-income individuals, racial and ethnic minorities, and those living in underserved areas.

    Problem*: Access to quality, affordable healthcare varies significantly, resulting in disparities in health outcomes.

    Common Misconception: It's easy to assume that people without health insurance simply choose not to have it, but this ignores the complex systemic barriers that prevent many from accessing affordable care. These barriers include poverty, lack of access to transportation, discriminatory practices, and the geographic maldistribution of healthcare resources.

    Roadblocks to Progress: Addressing healthcare inequality often involves systemic reforms such as expanding insurance coverage, increasing funding for community health centers, and addressing discriminatory practices. These changes can be politically charged, face resistance from vested interests, and require substantial financial investment.

    *National Institutes of Health - Minority Health and Health Disparities Research

The Problem with Systemic Problems

Systemic problems are complex because they're rarely caused by a single faulty component. Instead, the problem lies in the relationships and interactions between the parts. This makes solutions more challenging, requiring a deeper understanding of the whole system rather than just fixing individual elements.
